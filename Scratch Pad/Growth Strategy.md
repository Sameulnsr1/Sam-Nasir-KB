Market Maturity
This is where Disney+ is today. We are no longer scaling for growth at all costs. The streaming market is no longer a land grab.  Every net new subscriber we acquire is increasingly coming from a competitor, which means CAC is structurally higher and the acquisition pool is structurally smaller.
As a mature product, Growth is now an output of Activation, Engagement, Retention, and Monetization
Marketing’s fundamental goal now should be to drive retention. 
Marketing is a lever to create more liquidity so we can reinvest capital gained back into our growth strategy. 
Retention is the output of Engagement, Acquisition quality, and activation
Subscriber growth scales linearly with marketing spend. Churn scales exponentially as a percentage of your user base. 5% of 100k users vs. 5% of 1M users. This is why retention should be our goal across acquisition and lifecycle. 
Our Marketing Strategy is also still based on the premise of a funnel. Acquisition -> Activation -> Retention -> Revenue -> Referral. Why does this not work today?
Funnels lead us to believe in acquisition, retention, and monetization as silos rather than systems.
Our acquisition strategy is currently separate from our product and monetization model. They do not work together as a cohesive system. 
Funnels move in one direction. They have a start and an end
We put more on the top, and we will get more on the bottom. It leads us to believe we just need to keep putting more on top: more tactics, more channels.
This is unsustainable. We need to understand the connection of how we re-invest what comes from the bottom to get more growth on top
Our current north start metric for measuring Marketing performance is cost per market attributable sign up. This reinforces the point that we think of growth as a funnel and treat our business at the wrong stage of the SaaS product lifecycle.  
Retention and Engagement is what powers our acquisition and monetization, not the other way around.
For example: A user signs up and a certain percentage of users pay for the product, the revenue from the user is used to buy more ads. The greater percentage of users that I retain, the more money I’m going to earn from that cohort. This means I’ll have more money to reinvest back into marketing (liquidity)
Our lagging metrics to define growth should be:
Net Revenue Retention, which measures whether your existing subscriber base is growing or shrinking in revenue terms without any new acquisition
Payback Period or how quickly a user is profitable to the business. 
Our leading metric should be our retention metric, which the business has defined as Daily Active Users. This is what our growth strategy should strive for:  
How do we achieve a smile pattern for our retention curve, where we not only retain a flat percentage of users but we do a good job at reactivating users that started in our initial cohorts. 
Growth drives the same retention metrics and reinforces each input as a system that is repeatable, sustainable and defensible
What does this look like in practice?
Engagement depth (DAU/WAU) increases → cohort retention improves → Payback Period shortens → NRR crosses above 100% → excess revenue funds next acquisition cycle → acquisition is targeted at high-retention-propensity audiences → new subscribers onboard with higher engagement depth → DAU/WAU improves further → retention curve develops smile pattern → Payback Period shortens further → NRR compounds
Each part of the loop becomes a surface area that Marketing, Lifecycle, and Growth own to improve with the express goal of having an acquired subscriber be active daily. 
How can CDI help? By allowing us to supercharge our ability for experimentation and measure the performance of our strategies across each part of the loop.
